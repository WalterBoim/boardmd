QID 452
Vascular · Immunology
A 24-year-old Asian woman presents with a 6-month history of fatigue, low-grade fevers, and episodes of transient visual disturbances. On examination, blood pressure is undetectable in the right arm, a bruit is heard over the carotid arteries, and funduscopy shows arteriovenous anastomoses. ESR is elevated. Which diagnosis best explains this clinical picture?
A
Kawasaki disease
10%
B
Giant cell arteritis
10%
C
Takayasu arteritis
68%
D
Polyarteritis nodosa
12%
Correct Answer Takayasu arteritis
Wikimedia Commons — Takayasu ArteritisPublic domain
Explanation
Takayasu arteritis is a large-vessel granulomatous vasculitis predominantly affecting young Asian women, involving the aorta and its major branches. It presents with constitutional symptoms, upper extremity pulse deficits (hence "pulseless disease"), bruits, and visual and neurological symptoms from ischemia to the eyes and brain.
Why the Others are Wrong
Giant cell arteritis also affects large vessels with granulomatous inflammation but occurs in patients older than 50 and predominantly involves the temporal artery, making it unlikely in this young woman.
Polyarteritis nodosa is a medium-vessel vasculitis with fibrinoid necrosis that typically affects middle-aged men and spares the pulmonary arteries; it does not cause the upper extremity pulse asymmetry seen here.
Kawasaki disease is a medium-vessel vasculitis affecting children under 5, causing coronary artery aneurysms, and would not present in a young adult woman with aortic arch involvement.
Key Point
Takayasu arteritis (pulseless disease) presents in young women with upper extremity pulse deficits, bruits, and visual and neurological symptoms due to granulomatous inflammation of the aorta and its branches.
